# BIKE-SALES-ANALYSIS

## PROJECT OVERVIEW
1. This data analysis project aims to provide insight of a Bike Sales in different regions. By analyzing the Average income of customers that purchased a Bike in a particular region.
The Age/Age bracket of customers that purchased a bike in those region, and The Commute Distance of customers that purchased a bike. We seek to Identify Trends, Make data-driven decisions and gain a deeper understanding of Bike Sales in these various regions.

### DATA ANALYSIS
- Created Pivot Tables to summarize key variables such as average income, age distribution, and commute distance of customers.
- Used aggregation functions (e.g., Average, Count) within Pivot Tables to derive insights.
- Grouped customers into age brackets to better understand purchasing patterns.
- Analyzed relationships between income levels and bike purchases across different regions.
- Built visualizations such as charts (bar charts, column charts) to highlight trends and comparisons.



### Results/Findings
- Customers who purchased bikes tend to have higher average income compared to those who did not.
- Individuals in the middle-age range (approx. 30–50 years) are the most frequent buyers.
- Customers with shorter commute distances (0–5 miles) are more likely to purchase bikes.
- Bike purchases vary by region, with some regions (e.g., Europe/Pacific) showing higher adoption.
- Customers with fewer cars are more likely to purchase bikes, suggesting bikes may serve as an alternative transport option.
- Homeowners may show slightly higher purchase rates, indicating financial stability could influence buying decisions.


### Recommendations

- Focus on mid-income earners: They are the most responsive segment for bike purchases.
- Promote bikes for short commutes: Emphasize convenience and cost savings.
- Target urban regions with higher adoption rates for better ROI on marketing.
- Bundle offers for customers with fewer cars (position bikes as an alternative transport).
- Segment marketing by age group, especially targeting working-class adults.


### Limitation
- Decimal values in certain columns (e.g., income) were converted to whole numbers for consistency, which may slightly reduce the precision of the data.
- Some missing values were handled during the cleaning process, which may introduce minor bias depending on how they were treated.
- Duplicate records were removed, assuming they were errors, which may affect total counts if duplicates were valid entries.
- Certain columns that were not relevant to the analysis were excluded, which may limit deeper insights.
